The Inconspicuous Role of Intelligence Agencies in International Relations

Shreya Sreejesh

The word ‘intelligence agencies’ may bring about a dozen different pictures in one’s mind for those who are part of the order. Undercover agents, sophisticated gadgets, adrenaline heavy days, car chases and so much more shrouded in secrecy. Domestic marvels like Sherlock and international powerhouses like Bond become the epitome of espionage and intelligence gathering in popular media. However it is safe to say that intelligence agencies do not only work towards avoiding trickery at every corner of their operations but to supply a nation with crucial information relevant to securing the safety of its citizens all over the world. 

Unbiased, realistic, timely and accurate intelligence is key in successfully maintaining the security structure of a nation. Beyond the realm of security too, such information dissemination is essential in formulating foreign policy frameworks within a nation. Past experiences, present realities and future aspirations are better shaped with increasing access to any and all sorts of intel. The changing realities of globalisation coupled with unprecedented advancements in technology transfer, make a sound intelligence network the institutional core of handling such issues. The kind of intelligence transfer we observe is called human intelligence - with people to people transfer of information, where the source and extraction are both through human interaction. Apart from this, intelligence delves into interceptive intelligence - to tap into conversations over means of telecommunication, geospatial - capturing imagery through satellites and overall cyber security - to ensure maintenance of a hygienic digital interface and create impermeable servers. Strong intelligence agencies all over the world ask themselves the 5 universally important questions - what, who, when, why and how. Why does my nation require this information? Who will provide me with accurate information? When can I aim to acquire this information? and How will this information aid my nation in its future? Once these questions are answered, agencies all around the world go to intricate methods of information extraction and aim to facilitate the process seamlessly. The means many use go against official channels of communication, the idea of intelligence acquisition by this way is what gives leaders a certain edge above the rest to make apt policy decisions. 

Intelligence agencies help foreign policy makers in three ways. One, prior intel provision. Intelligence served even a second too late proves to be futile. Prior provision of the same helps to render factual information to the interested parties. This information can then be taken to alarm against future disasters, mitigate dangers, prevent damage and make overall ad hoc decision making a much easier process. Two, Holistic Suggestions. Taking a decision based on surface level information often fails to lead to an effective outcome. The chance of existing loopholes and future troubles become endless. Thus, with the presence of sharp intel, better policy decisions can be made by guiding the decision makers through the scope and variety of intricate issues that may arise under different policy options. Three, intricate evaluation. The final aid of intelligence agencies is to clearly establish the means and motives of any counterpart state, nation or organisation. This will make it easier for the possessor of relevant information to know exactly what to do with the information they have at hand. 

Through the course of this topic, one thing I realised for certain was the sheer lack of literature available on the subject. Given the nature of operations that intelligence agencies undertake, it is impossible to access any sort of reliable data on their operations, success stories, exact aid to organisations or even their concrete contributions to the workings of maintaining world peace and justice. All that is available is a broad idea of their existence through books written by former officers, memoirs, ‘whistle-blowing’ articles or books written to present historical contexts. There is no doubt that such ‘off-the-record’ organisations play their quiet role every single day to protect their own people and the world at large. While details of their workings may never see the light of day, it is important to leave them as much of a mention as they desire to ensure their immortal mark in the making of a just world order.
